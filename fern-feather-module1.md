## Practice 1: Extended Exhale Breathing

### The Science
Your vagus nerve — the main highway of your parasympathetic nervous system — is sensitive to the rhythm of your breath. Extending your exhale activates the "rest and digest" response, signaling safety to your brain even when your thoughts are still racing.

### How to Do It
1. Find a comfortable position (sitting or lying down)
2. Place one hand on your chest, one on your belly
3. Inhale through your nose for a count of 4
4. Exhale through your mouth for a count of 6-8 (aim for exhale longer than inhale)
5. Repeat for 10-15 cycles

## Practice 2: Cold Water Reset

### The Science
Cold water on your face triggers the mammalian dive reflex — an ancient biological response that slows heart rate and activates the parasympathetic nervous system. It's fast, free, and surprisingly effective.

### How to Do It
1. Fill a bowl with cold water (or use a cold, wet washcloth)
2. Splash cold water on your face 3-5 times, OR
3. Hold a cold, wet washcloth to your face for 30 seconds
4. Optional: Let some water drip down your wrists (more vagus nerve coverage)

### When to Use It
- As a morning "wake up" for a dysregulated nervous system
- Before a stressful meeting or difficult conversation
- When you feel anxiety rising and need an immediate reset

## Practice 3: Grounding Through Your Feet

### The Science
Anxiety often creates a "floating" sensation — feeling unmoored, dizzy, or disconnected from your body. Intentional foot-to-floor contact sends proprioceptive signals that literally ground you in the present moment.

### How to Do It
1. Stand with feet hip-width apart
2. Press your feet firmly into the floor (don't lock your knees)
3. Notice the texture of your socks or shoes, the temperature of the floor
4. Slowly shift weight: side to side, then front to back
5. Feel the four corners of each foot making contact
6. Breathe normally for 60 seconds while maintaining this awareness

## Practice 4: Butterfly Hug (Self-Hug with Bilateral Stimulation)

### The Science
Crossing your arms over your chest and tapping alternately on each shoulder provides bilateral stimulation — a technique used in EMDR therapy to help the brain process and calm distressing states. Combined with self-touch (which releases oxytocin), this is a powerful self-soothing tool.

### How to Do It
1. Cross your arms over your chest, hands on opposite shoulders
2. Notice the feeling of your hands on your shoulders
3. Apply gentle pressure — not too light, not too firm
4. Take 3 slow breaths while maintaining contact
5. Optional: Gently alternate tapping left, right, left, right while breathing

## Practice 5: Orienting to Your Environment

### The Science
When anxious, your visual field often narrows. Orienting — deliberately looking around your environment — activates your ventral vagal pathways and reminds your nervous system that you are safe right now, in this space.

### How to Do It
1. Pause what you're doing
2. Slowly turn your head to the right, letting your eyes land on something neutral
3. Name 1 color you see
4. Slowly turn to the left, let your eyes land on something else
5. Name 1 texture you see
6. Repeat 3-5 times, allowing your neck to move easily
7. Notice any sounds, scents, or temperature changes
