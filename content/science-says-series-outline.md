## Series Concept

Short-form video series debunking common therapy myths, explaining complex neuroscience in accessible ways, and clarifying where popular psychology gets it wrong (or oversimplified).

**Format:** 60-90 second talking head videos
**Tone:** Smart but approachable, slightly sarcastic, science-backed but never condescending
**Visuals:** Green screen text overlays for key terms, occasional diagrams via props/printed visuals

## Episode Guide

### Episode 1: "Toxic Positivity Isn't Just Annoying—It's Bad Science"
**Hook:** "'Just think positive' is asking your prefrontal cortex to override your amygdala. Spoiler: it doesn't work that way."
**The Science:**
- Prefrontal cortex vs limbic system function during stress
- The paradox of suppression (Wegner's white bear research)
- Why forcing positivity increases anxiety (Toxic Positivity Research, Quoidbach et al.)
**Myth Busted:** Positive thinking alone can cure depression/anxiety
**Key Takeaway:** Validation before regulation—acknowledge the feeling first
**CTA:** "Save this for the next person who tells you to 'just be grateful'"

---

### Episode 2: "Why 'Choose Happiness' Is Neurobiologically Impossible (Sometimes)"
**Hook:** "Your brain can't 'choose' anything when your nervous system is in shutdown mode."
**The Science:**
- Dorsal vagal shutdown vs ventral vagal connection (Polyvagal Theory, critically examined)
- Window of Tolerance (Siegel) — when the rational brain goes offline
- Executive function requires safety first (Perry's Neurosequential Model)
**Myth Busted:** Happiness is a choice you can make at any moment
**Key Takeaway:** You can't choose your way out of a biological survival state
**CTA:** "Tag someone who needs to hear that their brain isn't 'broken'"

---

### Episode 3: "Attachment Styles Aren't Personality Types"
**Hook:** "You're not 'just anxious.' You ADAPTED. And adaptation can change."
**The Science:**
- Attachment as survival strategy, not trait (Bowlby, Ainsworth, then Main/Fonagy)
- Neuroplasticity in attachment relationships (Cozolino's work)
- Earned secure attachment is real and achievable
**Myth Busted:** "I'm just avoidant, that's who I am"
**Key Takeaway:** Attachment is adaptation + relationship. Both can shift.
**CTA:** "Which myth about attachment do you hear most? Tell me below"

---

### Episode 4: "Trauma Isn't Stored in Your Body—But Your Body Remembers"
**Hook:** "Let's talk about what 'the body keeps the score' actually means (and where it gets oversimplified)."
**The Science:**
- Neuroception vs conscious memory (Porges)
- Procedural/emotional memories operate differently than explicit memory (LeDoux)
- The difference between body awareness and trauma storage
**Myth Busted:** Trauma is literally stored in your muscles/organs
**Key Takeaway:** The body remembers patterns, not events. That's why somatic work helps.
**CTA:** "Confused about polyvagal? I've got a whole nuanced take—link in bio"

---

### Episode 5: "The Vagus Nerve Is Important—But It's Not Magic"
**Hook:** "Your vagus nerve isn't a 'reset button.' It's a complex highway with multiple lanes."
**The Science:**
- Dorsal motor nucleus vs nucleus ambiguus pathways (different functions)
- Vagal tone vs vagal efficiency (what HRV actually measures)
- The vagus does more than 'calm you down'—it regulates social engagement too
**Myth Busted:** Cold water on your face "resets" your nervous system instantly
**Key Takeaway:** Vagal regulation is real, but more complex than TikTok suggests
**CTA:** ""Vagus nerve hack" videos are missing this entire branch of the nerve..."

---

### Episode 6: "Shame Isn't 'Low Self-Esteem'—It's a Neurological State"
**Hook:** "Shame doesn't respond to logic because it doesn't live in your thinking brain."
**The Science:**
- Shame as dorsal vagal shutdown (Schore's affect regulation research)
- The link between shame and the anterior insula (interoception failure)
- Why 'you have nothing to be ashamed of' doesn't help (Dickerson & Kemeny)
**Myth Busted:** Shame can be reasoned away with facts
**Key Takeaway:** Shame requires bottom-up (somatic) intervention, not top-down (cognitive)
**CTA:** "The words 'don't be ashamed' have never helped anyone—save this instead"

---

### Episode 7: "Your Gut Isn't Your 'Second Brain'—But It Talks to Your First One"
**Hook:** "90% of serotonin receptors are in your gut. Your digestive system IS part of your mental health."
**The Science:**
- Gut-brain axis via the vagus nerve
- Serotonin production in the gut vs brain (different functions—95% in gut, but it doesn't cross BBB)
- Microbiome influence on mood (emerging research—be careful not to oversell)
**Myth Busted:** Gut health can cure depression
**Key Takeaway:** Gut health matters for mood regulation, but it's one piece of a complex puzzle
**CTA:** "The gut-brain connection is real—but so is the 'we need more research' caveat"

---

### Episode 8: "ADHD Isn't a 'Lack of Discipline'—It's Dopamine Dysregulation"
**Hook:** "Your ADHD brain isn't lazy. It's hunting dopamine in a world built for neurotypicals."
**The Science:**
- Dopamine's role in motivation and reward prediction (not just 'pleasure')
- Delayed discounting in ADHD brains
- Task initiation requires dopamine—willpower isn't sufficient
**Myth Busted:** "You can focus on video games, so you can focus on anything"
**Key Takeaway:** Interest-based nervous systems need different strategies, not more discipline
**CTA:** "What's something you thought was 'laziness' that was actually neurobiology?"

---

### Episode 9: "Why Anxiety Isn't 'Just Worry'—It's Body-Based"
**Hook:** "Anxiety is your nervous system preparing for threat. Even when there's no actual threat."
**The Science:**
- The difference between worry (cognitive) and anxiety (physiological)
- Autonomic arousal without cognitive trigger (neuroception)
- Interoception—how your brain interprets body signals
**Myth Busted:** "It's all in your head" (it's actually in your nervous system)
**Key Takeaway:** Anxiety requires body-based interventions, not just talk therapy
**CTA:** "Save this for the next time someone tells you to 'just stop worrying'"

---

### Episode 10: "Complex Trauma Isn't 'Worse' Than Single-Event Trauma—It's Different"
**Hook:** "Complex PTSD isn't PTSD with extra credit. It's a different diagnosis for a different experience."
**The Science:**
- PTSD (DSM-5) vs C-PTSD (ICD-11) distinctions
- Developmental trauma vs shock trauma (different neural impacts)
- Why 'exposure therapy' works for single events but can harm complex trauma survivors
**Myth Busted:** All trauma should be treated the same way
**Key Takeaway:** The treatment must match the trauma type—one size harms all
**CTA:** "Did 'trauma therapy' not work for you? You might need a different approach"

---

### Episode 11: "Emotional Regulation Isn't 'Calming Down'—It's Flexible Responding"
**Hook:** "Regulation doesn't mean you're always calm. It means you can RESPOND, not just REACT."
**The Science:**
- The difference between suppression (unhealthy) and modulation (healthy)
- Window of Tolerance expansion, not elimination of dysregulation
- All emotions are information—all states are temporary
**Myth Busted:** "Regulated people don't get upset"
**Key Takeaway:** Regulation = flexibility, not constant zen
**CTA:** "Being 'calm' isn't the goal—being responsive is"

---

### Episode 12: "The 'Inner Child' Is Real—but It's Not Literal"
**Hook:** "IFS and inner child work aren't about actual children living in your brain."
**The Science:**
- Self-as-context vs parts (IFS, Schwartz—evidence base growing)
- Neural networks encoding early experiences
- State-dependent memory and ego states
**Myth Busted:** Inner child work is "woo-woo" regression therapy
**Key Takeaway:** Parts work is a framework for understanding different self-states
**CTA:** "IFS has more research support than you think—link in bio for resources"

---

### Episode 13: "Neuroplasticity Has Limits—And That's Okay"
**Hook:** "Yes, your brain can change. No, it can't change into anything at any time forever."
**The Science:**
- Experience-expectant vs experience-dependent plasticity
- Critical periods vs sensitive periods
- The role of safety and repetition in neural change
**Myth Busted:** "You can change your brain at any age, instantly, with the right mindset"
**Key Takeaway:** Change is real but requires conditions: safety, repetition, time
**CTA:** "Healing takes time because biology takes time—not because you're doing it wrong"

---

### Episode 14: "Co-Regulation Isn't Codependency—It's Biological Necessity"
**Hook:** "Humans are literally built to regulate nervous systems together. That's not weakness."
**The Science:**
- Polyvagal social engagement system
- Interpersonal neurobiology (Siegel)
- Co-regulation across the lifespan (not just infant-caregiver)
**Myth Busted:** "You should be able to self-regulate completely independently"
**Key Takeaway:** Needing others isn't pathology—it's being a mammal
**CTA:** "Independence is a myth. Interdependence is how we're built."

---

### Episode 15: "Meditation Isn't for Everyone—And That's Science Too"
**Hook:** "If meditation makes you more anxious, your nervous system isn't wrong. The practice might be."
**The Science:**
- Meditation-induced anxiety/depression (rare but real)
- Trauma-sensitive modifications necessary for some
- Open monitoring vs focused attention (different neural impacts)
**Myth Busting:** "Meditation works for everyone if you do it right"
**Key Takeaway:** Trauma-informed meditation requires adaptation—or alternatives
**CTA:** "Meditation is a tool, not a cure. And not the right tool for every job."

## Production Guide

### Visual Format
1. **Hook (0-3 sec):** Direct to camera, bold statement/question
2. **Myth Restatement (3-8 sec):** "You probably heard..."
3. **The Science (8-45 sec):** Explain the mechanism, use hand gestures, maybe hold up a printed diagram
4. **Myth Busted (45-55 sec):** Clear refutation
5. **Key Takeaway (55-65 sec):** What to know/do instead
6. **CTA (65-90 sec):** Engagement prompt

### Props/Visual Aids
- Printed brain diagram (simple—prefrontal cortex, amygdala, brainstem)
- Vagus nerve diagram (showing branches to organs)
- Hand-drawn window of tolerance chart on whiteboard
- Book references held up to camera (Cozolino, Porges, etc.)
