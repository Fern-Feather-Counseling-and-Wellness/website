## Main Article: Spring Nervous System Reset

### Why Spring Feels Exhausting (It's Not Just You)

Biologically, spring is a massive transition. Your body is responding to:
- **Increased daylight:** Longer days trigger hormonal changes (melatonin decreases, serotonin increases)
- **Temperature shifts:** Your cardiovascular system adapts to warmer weather
- **Allergens:** Pollen and other allergens can trigger inflammation that affects mood and energy
- **Social expectations:** "Spring energy" pressure can create stress when you don't feel it

But here's the key insight: **Your nervous system doesn't switch seasons like a light bulb.** It's a gradual adjustment that requires patience and the right conditions.

### The Three Phases of Spring Reset

Think of your spring reset in three phases — not as one big push:

#### Phase 1: Gentle Awakening (Weeks 1-2)

Your goal isn't productivity. It's **sensitivity**. Help your nervous system notice the changes happening:

- **Morning light exposure:** 5-10 minutes outside within an hour of waking (no sunglasses)
- **Movement exploration:** What does your body want? Slow stretching, gentle walks, not intense workouts
- **Sensory awareness:** Notice one new sign of spring each day — a bud, a bird sound, a scent

This phase is about turning the dial up slightly, not flipping a switch.

#### Phase 2: Building Momentum (Weeks 3-4)

Once your nervous system has adjusted to more light and movement, you can add more structured practices:

- **Rhythm creation:** Consistent wake-up and sleep times (your circadian rhythm loves predictability)
- **Movement variety:** Different types of movement throughout the week (strength, flexibility, cardio)
- **Social re-engagement:** Reaching out to one friend per week after winter's social hibernation

#### Phase 3: Sustainable Emergence (Month 2+)

This is where you find your new spring baseline:

- **Integration:** What spring routines actually feel good vs. what feels like obligation?
- **Energy budgeting:** How can you use your increased energy without overspending it?
- **Boundary setting:** Saying no to the spring activities that drain you

### The Danger of "Spring Productivity Pressure"

Social media is about to flood with "spring cleaning," "fitness challenges," and "quarterly goal setting." This creates what I call **seasonal performance anxiety** — the pressure to match your energy to the season's expectations.

Remember: **Your nervous system's needs come before the season's demands.**

If you're still feeling winter-level tired, that's information, not failure. Pushing through exhaustion because "it's spring" will only lead to burnout by summer.

### Three Gentle Spring Regulation Practices

#### 1. Grounded Growth Visualization

When you feel pressured to "do more" with spring energy:

1. Sit with your feet flat on the floor
2. Imagine yourself as a plant emerging from winter soil
3. Notice: Does growth feel forced or natural? Rushed or rhythmic?
4. Let your body show you its pace

#### 2. Seasonal Synchrony Breathing

A breathing pattern that mimics the gradual lengthening of spring days:

1. Inhale for 4 counts (winter breath)
2. Hold for 2 counts (transition)
3. Exhale for 6 counts (spring extension)
4. Repeat 5 times, gradually increasing exhale to 8 counts over several breaths

#### 3. Sensory Spring Mapping

Build your nervous system's tolerance for increased stimulation:

1. Choose one sense each day (sight, sound, smell, touch, taste)
2. Notice 3 spring-related inputs for that sense
3. Name them without judgment ("I see cherry blossoms" not "I should appreciate cherry blossoms")
4. Notice any activation or calm in your body

### When Spring Feels Like Anxiety

For some people, spring's increased light and activity triggers anxiety rather than energy. This is common if:

- Your nervous system associates activation with threat (trauma history)
- You're neurodivergent and sensory changes feel overwhelming
- You've been using winter's quiet to manage underlying anxiety

If spring feels triggering, not energizing:

1. **Honor your pace:** You don't have to match seasonal expectations
2. **Create buffers:** Sunglasses, ear protection, scheduling downtime
3. **Seek support:** A therapist can help you navigate seasonal transitions safely

### What This Means for You

Spring reset isn't about doing more. It's about **aligning differently.**

Instead of asking "What should I accomplish this spring?" try asking:
- What does my nervous system need to adjust to this season?
- What spring activities actually feel regulating (not just culturally expected)?
- How can I honor both my need for emergence and my need for rest?
