## What Is Trauma-Informed Therapy?

Trauma-informed therapy is an approach that assumes trauma is common and understands its widespread impact on individuals, families, and communities. Rather than asking "What's wrong with you?" a trauma-informed therapist asks "What happened to you?"

This shift in perspective changes everything:

**Traditional approach:** Focuses on symptoms and diagnoses
**Trauma-informed approach:** Focuses on understanding the root causes of symptoms

**Traditional approach:** Sees behavior as the problem
**Trauma-informed approach:** Sees behavior as adaptive survival strategies

**Traditional approach:** Therapy happens in the office, separate from context
**Trauma-informed approach:** Therapy acknowledges social, cultural, and systemic factors

## The Six Principles of Trauma-Informed Care

According to the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA), trauma-informed care follows six key principles:

### 1. Safety

Physical and emotional safety is prioritized. This includes:
- Clear boundaries and expectations
- Consistent scheduling and communication
- A therapy space that feels secure
- No surprises or unexpected interventions

### 2. Trustworthiness and Transparency

Decisions are made openly, with client input:
- You know what's happening and why
- Informed consent is ongoing, not just at intake
- Your therapist is clear about their training and limitations

### 3. Peer Support

Healing happens in connection:
- Group therapy options when appropriate
- Recognition that you're not alone
- Shared experiences are valued

### 4. Collaboration and Mutuality

You are the expert on your own life:
- Treatment planning is collaborative
- Your goals drive the work
- Power imbalances are acknowledged and minimized

### 5. Empowerment, Voice, and Choice

Your agency is central:
- You choose your goals
- You choose your pace
- You can say no to any intervention
- Your strengths are identified and built upon

### 6. Cultural, Historical, and Gender Issues

Context matters:
- Your cultural background is considered
- Historical trauma is acknowledged
- Gender-responsive approaches are used
- Systemic oppression is recognized

## What Trauma-Informed Therapy Looks Like in Practice

### Assessment

Rather than jumping to a diagnosis, a trauma-informed therapist will:
- Ask about your history in a way that doesn't retraumatize
- Understand current symptoms in context
- Assess your window of tolerance and coping resources
- Consider cultural and systemic factors

### Treatment Planning

Together, you'll create a plan that:
- Prioritizes safety and stabilization
- Moves at your pace
- Includes your goals and preferences
- Builds on your strengths

### Interventions

Trauma-informed therapists use evidence-based approaches like:

**EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing):** Helps process traumatic memories without requiring detailed verbal processing.

**Somatic Experiencing:** Works with the body's trauma responses to release stored stress.

**Internal Family Systems (IFS):** Helps you understand and heal protective parts that developed in response to trauma.

**Trauma-Focused CBT:** Adapts cognitive-behavioral techniques specifically for trauma survivors.

## How to Find a Trauma-Informed Therapist in Alpharetta

### What to Look For

**Training:** Look for specific trauma training beyond general licensure:
- EMDR certification
- Somatic Experiencing training
- Trauma-focused CBT certification
- IFS Level 1 or higher

**Approach:** Ask potential therapists:
- "What does trauma-informed mean to you?"
- "How do you create safety in your office?"
- "What if I'm not ready to talk about what happened?"

**Fit:** The most important factor is whether you feel safe with this person. Trust your gut.

## The Science Behind Trauma-Informed Care

Trauma doesn't just affect the mind — it changes the brain and body:

**The amygdala** (threat detector) becomes hypersensitive, leading to anxiety and hypervigilance.

**The hippocampus** (memory processing) is affected, causing fragmented memories or flashbacks.

**The prefrontal cortex** (rational thinking) goes offline during stress, making it hard to think clearly when triggered.

**The nervous system** adapts to chronic threat, leading to either chronic activation (anxiety) or shutdown (depression).

Trauma-informed therapy works with these biological realities rather than against them. It recognizes that "just think positive" doesn't work when your brain is literally structured around threat detection.

## Is Trauma-Informed Therapy Right for You?

Consider trauma-informed therapy if you:

- Have experienced events that still affect you (big T or little t trauma)
- Struggle with anxiety, depression, or PTSD symptoms
- Have difficulty trusting others or maintaining relationships
- Feel stuck in patterns you can't explain
- Have tried traditional therapy without success
- Want to understand the "why" behind your symptoms

## The Bottom Line

Trauma-informed therapy isn't about dwelling on the past — it's about understanding how the past shapes your present so you can create a different future. For Alpharetta residents seeking mental health support, this approach offers a path to healing that honors your whole experience.
