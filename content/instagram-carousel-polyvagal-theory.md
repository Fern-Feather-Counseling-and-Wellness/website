## Slide-by-Slide Content

### Slide 1: Title Slide
**Headline:** Polyvagal Theory Explained
**Subhead:** Your body's 3-state threat detection system
**Visual:** Simple illustration of ladder with three rungs
**Caption Hook:** "Why do you sometimes feel anxious, numb, or calm? Polyvagal theory explains your nervous system's three survival states."

### Slide 2: The Three States
**Headline:** Your nervous system has 3 possible states
**Visual:** Three icons side by side:
1. **Ventral Vagal** — smiling sun icon (Safe & Social)
2. **Sympathetic** — lightning bolt icon (Fight/Flight)
3. **Dorsal Vagal** — turtle icon (Freeze/Shutdown)
**Text:** "These aren't emotions — they're biological survival responses"

### Slide 3: Ventral Vagal (Safe & Social)
**Headline:** The "green zone" — Safe & Connected
**Visual:** Person smiling, connecting with others, calm environment
**Key Features:**
- ✅ Heart rate regulated
- ✅ Social engagement possible
- ✅ Digestion works properly
- ✅ Clear thinking
**Text:** "This is where healing happens. Your body feels safe enough to connect, digest, and think clearly."

### Slide 4: Sympathetic (Fight/Flight)
**Headline:** The "yellow zone" — Alert & Activated
**Visual:** Person with wide eyes, tense body, looking around
**Key Features:**
- ⚠️ Heart rate increases
- ⚠️ Muscles tense
- ⚠️ Breathing shallow/rapid
- ⚠️ Hypervigilance
**Text:** "Your body thinks there's danger. Energy mobilizes for action — anxiety, panic, irritability."

### Slide 5: Dorsal Vagal (Freeze/Shutdown)
**Headline:** The "red zone" — Numb & Disconnected
**Visual:** Person curled up, looking away, disconnected
**Key Features:**
- 🔴 Energy conservation
- 🔴 Numbness/dissociation
- 🔴 Collapse/shutdown
- 🔴 Depression-like state
**Text:** "When fighting or fleeing isn't possible, your nervous system shuts down to conserve energy."

### Slide 6: How States Shift
**Headline:** You move between states constantly
**Visual:** Animated arrow moving up/down the ladder
**Text:**
- **Triggers move you down** (stress, trauma reminders, overwhelm)
- **Resources move you up** (safety, connection, regulation tools)
- **The goal isn't to stay in ventral always**
- **The goal is flexible movement between states**
**Key Insight:** "Health isn't staying calm — it's being able to return to calm after stress."

### Slide 7: Practical Applications
**Headline:** How this helps in therapy & daily life
**Visual:** Person practicing grounding techniques
**Practical Tips:**
1. **Name your state** — "I'm in sympathetic right now"
2. **Meet yourself there** — don't fight it, acknowledge it
3. **Use regulation tools** — breathing, grounding, connection
4. **Build ventral resources** — safety, pleasure, play

Polyvagal theory gives us a map for understanding our nervous system's responses to stress and threat. 🧭

Instead of judging ourselves as "anxious" or "depressed," we can recognize: "My nervous system is in sympathetic (fight/flight) mode" or "I've shifted into dorsal vagal (shutdown)."

This isn't about labeling or pathologizing — it's about understanding our biology so we can work with it, not against it.

At Fern & Feather, we use polyvagal theory as one of many tools to help clients:
• Understand their physiological responses
• Develop personalized regulation strategies
• Build resilience in their nervous system
• Move toward more flexible state-shifting

## Scientific Context Note

**For therapist audience/if questions arise:**
Polyvagal theory (Porges, 1994) has faced methodological criticism in recent years (Grossman et al., 2026). We present it as:
- A **clinical heuristic** for understanding nervous system states
- **Not** established biological fact
- **One tool among many** in trauma therapy
- Focus on **practical applications** rather than biological claims

**Recommended response to scientific questions:**
"Polyvagal theory is a useful clinical model that helps clients understand their nervous system responses. Like all models, it has limitations and the science continues to evolve. We find it valuable for helping people make sense of their physiological experiences, while staying informed about ongoing research."
