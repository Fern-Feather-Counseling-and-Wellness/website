### What Is Somatic Therapy?

Somatic therapy is a body-centered approach to mental health that recognizes the connection between your physical body and emotional wellbeing. The word "somatic" comes from the Greek "soma," meaning body.

Unlike traditional talk therapy, which focuses primarily on thoughts and conversations, somatic therapy pays attention to:

- Physical sensations in your body
- Nervous system patterns
- Muscle tension and posture
- Breath patterns
- Automatic physical responses

**The core principle:** Your body stores experiences—especially traumatic ones—that your mind may have processed but your nervous system hasn't fully released.

---

### How Does Somatic Therapy Work?

Your nervous system has two main branches that somatic therapy addresses:

**1. The Sympathetic Nervous System (Fight or Flight)**
When you're threatened, this system activates to help you respond. If you've experienced trauma or chronic stress, this system can get "stuck on," leading to:
- Racing thoughts
- Constant vigilance
- Anxiety and panic
- Difficulty sleeping
- Irritability

**2. The Parasympathetic Nervous System (Rest and Digest)**
This system helps you recover and relax. However, when overwhelmed, some people experience a "freeze" response—the parasympathetic system becomes dominant, causing:
- Numbness or disconnection
- Fatigue and exhaustion
- Brain fog
- Feeling "checked out"

**Somatic therapy helps you:**
- Recognize which state you're in
- Complete survival responses that got interrupted
- Build capacity to handle stress without shutting down or panicking
- Develop practical tools for regulation

### What Happens in a Somatic Therapy Session?

If you're considering somatic therapy in Alpharetta, here's what you can expect:

**The Beginning:** Your therapist will still talk with you about what brings you in. But instead of staying in conversation, they'll guide you to notice what's happening in your body as you share.

**You might hear questions like:**
- "As you're telling me about that, what do you notice in your body?"
- "Where do you feel that anxiety—chest, stomach, throat?"
- "What happens to your breath when you think about that memory?"

**The Middle:** Based on what you notice, your therapist will introduce body-based techniques:

- **Grounding exercises** to help you feel present and safe
- **Tracking sensations** to follow what your body is doing moment-to-moment
- **Titration**—touching on difficult material briefly, then resourcing (finding safety)
- **Pendulation**—moving between difficult sensations and comfortable ones
- **Movement or posture adjustments** to release held tension

**The End:** Sessions typically close with stabilization—ensuring you leave feeling grounded, not triggered.

### Who Can Benefit From Somatic Therapy?

Somatic therapy in Alpharetta may be especially helpful if you experience:

**Anxiety and Panic**
When anxiety lives in your body (racing heart, shallow breathing, muscle tension), talking about it only goes so far. Somatic approaches teach your nervous system to recognize safety.

**Trauma and PTSD**
Traditional therapy processes trauma through memory and narrative. Somatic therapy also addresses the physiological imprint—helping your body understand the threat is over.

**Chronic Stress and Burnout**
High-functioning anxiety often masks a dysregulated nervous system. Somatic work helps high achievers learn to rest without shutting down.

**Attachment Issues**
Early relational experiences shape our nervous systems. Somatic therapy can help repair attachment patterns through co-regulation with the therapist and new embodied experiences.

**When Talk Therapy Hasn't Worked**
If you've done years of therapy but still feel stuck, your body may be holding what conversation couldn't reach.

### Is Somatic Therapy Evidence-Based?

Research supports somatic approaches for trauma treatment:

- **Van der Kolk (2014)** found body-based interventions helped trauma survivors who didn't respond to talk therapy alone
- Clinical trials show Somatic Experiencing reduces PTSD symptoms significantly
- Neuroscience research confirms trauma is stored in the body, not just memory

However, somatic therapy is considered an emerging modality—less studied than CBT or EMDR, but growing in recognition and research support.

### What to Expect: The First Few Sessions

**Session 1-2:** Assessment and building safety. Your therapist learns your history and helps you develop body awareness without diving into difficult material.

**Sessions 3-6:** Establishing resources. You'll practice grounding and regulation techniques that become your foundation.

**Sessions 7+:** Processing work. As your nervous system stabilizes, you'll begin addressing deeper patterns with your therapist's guidance.

**Important:** Somatic therapy may feel unfamiliar if you're used to traditional talk therapy. The goal isn't to analyze—it's to experience safety in your body.
