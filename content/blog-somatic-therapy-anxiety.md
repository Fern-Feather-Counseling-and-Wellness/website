# Somatic Therapy for Anxiety: Releasing Tension, Finding Calm

*How working with your body can unlock relief from persistent anxiety*

---

## Understanding Anxiety Through the Body

Anxiety is more than just racing thoughts or a racing heart. It's a deeply physical experience that can manifest in countless ways:
- Persistent muscle tension (neck, shoulders, jaw)
- Shallow breathing or breath-holding
- Digestive issues (stomach aches, IBS)
- Fatigue and exhaustion
- Headaches and migraines
- Difficulty sleeping
- Feeling on edge or hypervigilous

For many, traditional talk therapy can be helpful but may not fully address the body's stored tension and survival responses that often fuel anxiety. This is where **somatic therapy** offers a powerful pathway to healing.

---

## What is Somatic Therapy?

Somatic therapy comes from the Greek word "soma," meaning "body." It's an umbrella term for a range of therapeutic approaches that focus on the mind-body connection. Instead of solely focusing on thoughts and behaviors, somatic therapy attends to the body's sensations, movements, and physiological responses as a direct pathway to understanding and releasing emotional distress, including anxiety (Ogden et al., 2006).

Somatic therapists believe that the body "remembers" and stores experiences, especially traumatic ones, in the form of physical tension, energy blockages, and self-protective patterns. These patterns, while once adaptive, can continue to trigger anxiety long after the initial threat has passed.

## How Somatic Therapy Helps Anxiety

Somatic therapy helps you explore and process anxiety by:

### 1. Increasing Body Awareness (Interoception)
Anxiety often involves a disconnect from bodily sensations. Somatic therapy helps you gently tune into your internal landscape—noticing subtle changes in your body without judgment. This increased awareness, or interoception, is the first step to understanding what your body needs when it's anxious (Smith, 2017).

### 2. Releasing Stored Tension
Your body's natural response to stress and threat is to tense up, often in preparation for fight, flight, or freeze. If this response becomes chronic, it can manifest as persistent anxiety. Somatic techniques, like gentle movements, breathwork, and mindful attention to sensation, help release this trapped physical energy (Levine, 2010).

### 3. Regulating the Nervous System
Somatic therapy works directly with the autonomic nervous system (ANS), which controls your body's stress response. By guiding you through nervous system regulation techniques, somatic therapists help you shift from a state of hyperarousal (fight/flight) or hypoarousal (freeze/fawn) back to a state of balance and calm—your "window of tolerance" (Siegel, 1999).

### 4. Processing Traumatic Memories
Anxiety is often rooted in past experiences, particularly those that felt overwhelming or threatening. Somatic approaches help you process these memories by attending to the bodily felt sense associated with them, allowing for their integration and resolution without re-traumatization (van der Kolk, 2014).

## Evidence-Based Somatic Approaches for Anxiety

Several evidence-based somatic modalities are highly effective for anxiety:

### Somatic Experiencing (SE)
Developed by Dr. Peter Levine, SE focuses on releasing the impact of trauma and stress stored in the body. It uses gentle attention to bodily sensations and movements to help individuals complete their natural "fight or flight" response and restore resilience (Levine, 2010).

**Research Status:** Growing evidence base for PTSD and anxiety disorders.

### Sensorimotor Psychotherapy
Co-created by Dr. Pat Ogden, this approach integrates somatic, cognitive, and emotional processing. It emphasizes the body's role in emotional regulation and offers techniques to address the postural, expressive, and movement patterns associated with anxiety and trauma (Ogden et al., 2006).

**Research Status:** Strong theoretical foundation with emerging empirical support.

### Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR)
While primarily known for trauma processing, EMDR also uses bilateral stimulation (e.g., eye movements) that affects the integration of memory and can help regulate the nervous system, making it effective for anxiety symptoms linked to specific distressing memories (Shapiro, 2017).

**Research Status:** Well-established for PTSD, with growing evidence for anxiety disorders.

### Trauma-Informed Yoga
Developed by clinicians and yoga therapists, this practice emphasizes choice, agency, and gentle movement. It helps individuals reconnect with their bodies, notice sensations without being overwhelmed, and build resilience—all crucial for managing anxiety (Emerson & Hopper, 2011).

**Research Status:** Promising results for PTSD and complex trauma, directly applicable to anxiety.
