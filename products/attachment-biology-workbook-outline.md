# Attachment Biology Workbook

**From Trauma Through Polyvagal Theory &amp; Somatic Strategies**

---

## Introduction &amp; Purpose

### Who Is This Workbook For?
**This workbook is for people who:**
- Grew up in emotionally unsafe environments
- Find themselves repeating painful patterns in relationships
- Want to understand *why* they react certain ways to intimacy, conflict, or closeness
- Are tired of toxic relationships and ready to break cycles

### Why "Attachment Biology"?
Attachment styles are often taught as personality traits — this workbook treats them as *biological survival patterns* rooted in your autonomic nervous system. Understanding how your body learned to protect you can help you build new, secure ways of relating.

This approach integrates **disorganized/fearful-avoidant** attachment as a distinct category — because some survivors' nervous systems learned that closeness *is* danger, and their body knows both how to reach for connection and how to flee from it.

### How to Use This Workbook
1. **Go at your own pace.** Your nervous system will tell you when you need breaks.
2. **Emotional content will arise.** That's normal — you're doing work that's been waiting a long time.
3. **Honor your limits.** You can always circle back to exercises later.
4. **Consider working with a therapist.** Somatic work can bring up material that benefits from professional support.

## Session 1: Understanding Your Nervous System

### Core Concept
**Polyvagal Theory** — the science of how your autonomic nervous system shapes your relationship to safety, connection, and threat. Your body learned strategies to survive long before you could think about them.

The three primary states:

#### 1. Ventral Vagal (Safe &amp; Connected)
- **Biology:** Social engagement system active — face muscles relaxed, voice tones soften, heart rate slows
- **Attachment expression:** Can receive comfort, pursue connection, tolerate conflict without shutdown
- **Internal experience:** Present, grounded, able to think clearly, able to feel both self and other

#### 2. Sympathetic (Mobilized &amp; Activated)
- **Biology:** Adrenaline and cortisol rise, heart rate increases, pupils dilate
- **Attachment expression:** Pursuit, anxiety, protest behaviors, anger, demands for reassurance
- **Internal experience:** Restlessness, urgency, rumination, "I need them to fix this NOW"

#### 3. Dorsal Vagal (Collapsed &amp; Shutdown)
- **Biology:** Metabolism slows, dissociation increases, body becomes numb or heavy
- **Attachment expression:** Withdrawal, ghosting, emotional unavailability, "I don't need anyone"
- **Internal experience:** Foggy, disconnected, hopeless, "Nothing matters anyway"

### The Autonomic Hierarchy
Safety and connection (ventral) is the foundation. When that fails, your system mobilizes (sympathetic). When mobilization doesn't work or isn't safe, it collapses (dorsal).

**Important:** You are not "broken" — your system is doing exactly what it learned was necessary.

### Daily Check-In Exercise
Track your nervous system state over one week. Notice patterns around conflict, intimacy, or stress.

**Example format:**
- **Time:** Morning coffee routine
- **Trigger:** Partner seemed distant
- **State:** Sympathetic (heartrate up, checking texts compulsively)
- **Response:** Sent 4 messages, then felt ashamed
- **Shift:** Took a walk, felt a bit of ventral return

## Session 2: Your Personal Attachment Map

### The Four Attachment Styles from a Polyvagal Lens

#### Secure Attachment
- **Nervous system trust:** Others can be a resource under stress; I can co-regulate
- **Expected internal state:** Primarily ventral vagal with quick recovery after threat
- **Growth indicator:** Can self-regulate AND seek co-regulation appropriately
- **Self-compassion note:** Secure attachment is a practice, not a personality — all states are available to everyone

#### Anxious-Preoccupied Attachment
- **Nervous system pattern:** Sympathetic dominance when attachment feels threatened
- **Core survival strategy:** When closeness feels uncertain, activate (protest, pursue, monitor, demand)
- **Biological logic:** "If I keep seeking connection, someone might finally come. The danger is *abandonment.*"
- **Common body cues:** Racing thoughts, chest tightness, texting compulsively, inability to focus
- **Growth question:** *How do I know when my nervous system reads uncertainty as threat?*

#### Avoidant-Dismissive Attachment
- **Nervous system pattern:** Dorsal vagal response to attachment pressure
- **Core survival strategy:** When demands for closeness feel overwhelming, collapse or shut down
- **Biological logic:** "Dependency was dangerous or disappointing. The danger is *being swallowed or trapped.*"
- **Common body cues:** Numbness, foggy thinking, needing space suddenly, "I don't care" feelings
- **Growth question:** *How can I notice the urge to withdraw *before* I've completely disappeared?*

#### Disorganized/Fearful-Avoidant (The Addition)
- **Nervous system pattern:** *Both* sympathetic AND dorsal vagal activation — sometimes cycling between them, sometimes both at once
- **Core survival strategy:** When closeness itself triggers fear (because early caregivers were both source of safety AND source of danger), you learn conflicting survival responses
- **Biological logic:** "The person I need is also the person I fear. I reach and flee at the same time."
- **Common body cues:** Shame spirals, confusion about what you want, "Come close — no wait, go away" with same person
- **Unique challenge:** Co-regulation feels threatening sometimes, AND co-regulation feels essential — creating an impossible bind
- **Growth question:** *Can I bring compassion to the part of me that learned love = danger?*

### Bonus Pattern: Anxious-Avoidant
- **Nervous system pattern:** Both sympathetic AND dorsal are familiar — you oscillate or alternate between them
- **What shifts:** Stress, time in relationship, partner's availability
- **Growth work:** Identify *which* state is dominant under which conditions

## Session 3: How Your Attachment Pattern Was Built

### The Biology of Early Attachment

Before you had language, your nervous system learned how to answer these questions:
1. Is anyone noticing my needs?
2. When I'm distressed, does someone show up?
3. Is closeness safe, or is closeness dangerous?

Your caregivers didn't need to be perfect — they needed to be "good enough" at attending to you. But if they were inconsistent, intrusive, critical, overwhelmed, or frightening themselves, your system built defenses that made sense *then* but may not serve you *now*.
