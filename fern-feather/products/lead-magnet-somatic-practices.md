# Pause the Panic
## 5 Somatic Practices for Immediate Anxiety Relief

*A trauma-informed guide by Fern & Feather Wellness Center*

---

**About This Guide**

Anxiety isn't just in your head—it's in your body. When your nervous system detects threat (real or perceived), it triggers a cascade of physical responses: racing heart, shallow breathing, muscle tension, the urge to freeze or flee.

The good news? You have more control than you think.

This guide gives you five evidence-based somatic (body-based) techniques that work with your nervous system, not against it. No meditation app required. No hour-long yoga class. Just you, your breath, and a few minutes.

These practices are drawn from **polyvagal theory**, **somatic experiencing**, and **trauma-informed therapy**. They're designed to be:
- **Fast** (most take 2–5 minutes)
- **Discreet** (use them in a bathroom stall, your car, or at your desk)
- **Effective** (grounded in nervous system science, not woo-woo)

---

## The Science in Plain English

Your autonomic nervous system has three main states:

| State | What It Feels Like | What Your Body Is Doing |
|-------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **Ventral Vagal (Safe & Social)** | Calm, connected, creative | You can breathe deeply, think clearly, engage with others |
| **Sympathetic (Fight or Flight)** | Anxious, restless, activated | Racing heart, shallow breathing, muscle tension |
| **Dorsal Vagal (Shutdown)** | Numb, disconnected, "checked out" | Low energy, brain fog, difficulty engaging |

**Anxiety = sympathetic activation.** These practices help you shift from "fight or flight" back toward "safe and social."

*Note: Recent research continues to refine polyvagal theory. While the core concept of autonomic flexibility (your ability to move between states) remains evidence-based, stay curious about evolving science. We're committed to science-based, not science-dogmatic, care.*

## Practice 1: The Physiological Sigh
**Time:** 60 seconds | **Best for:** Immediate overwhelm, shallow breathing

### What It Is
A double inhale followed by a long exhale—the fastest way to off-load carbon dioxide and activate your parasympathetic nervous system.

### How to Do It
1. Sit or stand with both feet on the ground
2. Take a sharp inhale through your nose
3. Take a second, shorter "topping up" inhale (sip in more air)
4. Exhale slowly and fully through your mouth, making a soft "haaa" sound if possible
5. Repeat 1–3 times

### Why It Works
The double inhale maximally expands your alveoli (air sacs) in the lungs. The long exhale signals safety to your brain, slows your heart rate, and clears excess CO2.

### When to Use It
- Before a difficult conversation
- During a panic spike
- When you realize you've been holding your breath
- Right now (seriously—try it)

## Practice 2: Grounding Through Your Feet
**Time:** 2–3 minutes | **Best for:** Dissociation, dizziness, feeling "in your head"

### What It Is
A standing or seated practice that brings your awareness into your lower body and physical contact with the ground.

### How to Do It
1. Stand with feet hip-width apart (or sit with both feet flat)
2. Rock gently forward onto the balls of your feet, then back onto your heels
3. Find the center point where weight distributes evenly
4. Notice the sensation of your feet in shoes or on floor
5. Gradually shift attention up through ankles, calves, knees
6. Notice how your legs hold you up without you micromanaging them

### Variations
- **Seated version:** Press feet firmly into floor, notice the resistance
- **Advanced:** Add a gentle knee bend, feel your leg muscles engage
- **Discreet version:** Do this while waiting in line—nobody will know

### Why It Works
Gravity is reliable. Your feet are always there. This practice uses **bottom-up regulation** (body → brain) to signal safety through sensory input and spatial awareness.

## Practice 3: Hand-on-Heart + Hand-on-Belly
**Time:** 2 minutes | **Best for:** Self-soothing before sleep, emotional flooding

### What It Is
A gentle self-touch practice that combines tactile comfort with breath awareness.

### How to Do It
1. One hand on your heart (center of chest)
2. One hand on your belly (below navel)
3. Close your eyes if that's comfortable
4. Feel the warmth of your hands
5. Notice: Does your belly hand move when you breathe? It should.
6. If breath is shallow, invite it deeper—not forcing, just inviting
7. Optional: Silently say "I am here" or "I am safe"

### Why It Works
- **Oxytocin release:** Self-touch stimulates the same hormone as hugging
- **Interoceptive awareness:** Feeling internal sensations builds body trust
- **Parasympathetic activation:** Heart + belly contact signals safety

### When to Use It
- Difficulty falling asleep
- After a triggering conversation
- Waking up anxious at 3 AM
- When you need to feel "held" but you're alone

## Practice 4: Orienting (The 5-4-3-2-1 Somatic Remix)
**Time:** 3–5 minutes | **Best for:** Acute anxiety, dissociation, trauma triggers

### What It Is
A modified 5-4-3-2-1 technique that adds intentional head movement to engage your orienting reflex—a built-in survival mechanism that helps mammals locate threat and safety.

### How to Do It
1. **5 things you see:** Name them slowly. Include colors, shapes, textures.
2. *Add head movement:* Turn your head slowly from left to right, letting your eyes track naturally.
3. **4 things you hear:** Notice sounds near and far. Don't strain—just receive.
4. *Add head movement:* Tilt your ear toward one shoulder, then the other.
5. **3 things you touch:** Feel textures—clothing, chair, your own skin.
6. **2 things you smell:** Even faint scents count (laundry detergent, air).
7. **1 thing you taste:** The inside of your mouth, lingering flavors.

### Why the Head Movement Matters
Predators move their heads to scan. Prey move their heads to watch. Your orienting reflex is ancient and powerful. Moving your head *intentionally while safe* helps your nervous system complete a threat-scan and register: "I see my environment. I can track movement. I am safe enough to pay attention."

### When to Use It
- Feeling "frozen" or dissociated
- After a flashback or intrusive memory
- When anxiety feels paralyzing, not just activating
- Anytime you need to "return" to the present moment

## Practice 5: Titrated Tapping (EFT Light)
**Time:** 3–5 minutes | **Best for:** Racing thoughts, intrusive worries, physical anxiety

### What It Is
A simplified version of Emotional Freedom Technique (EFT) that focuses on the karate chop point and collarbone tap. No setup statement required.

### How to Do It
1. **Setup:** Cross your arms over your chest, hands on upper arms
    - Or: Use fingertips to tap the "karate chop" edge of your hand (below pinky)
2. Tap steadily, about 1–2 taps per second
3. While tapping, name what you're feeling (out loud or silently):
    - "This anxiety in my chest"
    - "Racing thoughts about work"
    - "I'm really overwhelmed right now"
    - "My heart is pounding"
4. After 30–60 seconds, pause
5. Take a breath. Notice: Has the intensity shifted? Stay curious.
6. If still elevated, continue tapping, using updated language:
    - "Remaining anxiety..."
    - "Still worried but taking care of myself..."
    - "This anxious part of me needs attention..."

### Why It Works
Tapping sends calming signals through your nervous system via acupressure points. Pairing it with naming ("I see you, anxiety") prevents suppression and helps the emotion process through.

### Important Note
If naming the anxiety makes it worse, *don't*. Just tap and breathe. This is a flexible tool, not a rigid protocol.

## Building a Practice: What Actually Works

**The 2-Minute Rule:** If you can't do a full practice, do one round of physiological sighs. Something > nothing.

**Habit Stacking:** Attach a practice to an existing habit:
- Sighs before your first sip of coffee
- Foot grounding while brushing teeth
- Heart-hand on belly before opening your phone in bed

**Track What Works:** Everyone's nervous system is different. Notice which practices feel most regulating *for you* and lean into those.

**Compassion, Not Perfection:** Some days you'll forget. Some days you'll try a practice and it won't work. That's data, not failure. Come back tomorrow.

## When to Seek Additional Support

These practices are powerful but not a replacement for therapy. Consider reaching out to a professional if:

- Anxiety interferes with daily functioning (work, relationships, sleep)
- You're using substances to cope
- You have thoughts of self-harm
- You experience panic attacks regularly
- Trauma memories are intrusive or destabilizing
- You've tried self-regulation and still feel stuck

**You deserve support. These tools are a starting point, not the whole journey.**
